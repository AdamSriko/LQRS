# Setting: Qahwtea

**Type:** Specialty Coffee Café / Reality Show Set **Location:** Corner of Research Boulevard and Meridian Street, Huntsville, Alabama **Established:** ~4 years ago, independently owned

---

## Overview

Qahwtea (pronounced like "qahwa" — Arabic for coffee — blended with "tea") occupies the ground floor of a converted brick building between a coworking space and a lunch spot that has been "about to close" for fourteen months. The name confuses first-timers. Regulars consider navigation of this confusion a rite of passage. It appears on zero tourist lists and exactly one extremely well-curated Instagram account — which is not a coincidence.

The space is small enough that nothing is private and large enough that you can pretend it is. This is the essential condition of the drama.

The cast is the staff. Four baristas work the floor — Adam, Moe, Dianara, Heba — and Lavanya runs social media, which in practice means she runs the room. Everyone is always within three feet of someone else. Everyone is always being watched, even when the phone is down.

---

## The Two Stages

Qahwtea has two halves, and the show's spatial grammar lives in the difference between them.

**Front Stage** is the public-facing L of the bar — the Espresso Bar, the Register, the Dessert Fridge. This is where characters perform. The lobby can see them. Lavanya can film them. Orders are called, jokes land or don't, and small failures become public events in real time.

**Back Stage** is the deeper end of the room — the Traditional Bar, the Juice Bar, and the Sinks. Customers can technically see back here if they crane, but the sightline is bad and the ambient noise is worse. Things said at the Sinks do not exist on the record. Things said at the Espresso Bar are content.

The distance between Front and Back is about eight feet. The psychological distance is the entire show.

---

## The Stations

**The Espresso Bar (Front Stage — The Cockpit):** The heart of everything. A 12-foot marble-effect counter anchored by a La Marzocco Linea Classic — brushed chrome, one visible scratch at the back from a disputed incident no one will explain on camera. The machine is treated with the deference due a surgical instrument. It has a specific sound when it's running exactly right versus running under pressure. Heba hears the difference first. Adam hears it second and apologizes to the machine when he hears it. Behind the bar, there is room for two people to work. There is not comfortable room for two people to work. This is also a dramaturgical condition.

**The Register (Front Stage — The Social Gateway):** The point of contact with the lobby. Whoever is on register is the face of the shift for the next customer through the door, which means whoever is on register is performing whether they want to be or not. Dianara thrives here. Moe survives here. Adam treats it like a station he's politely visiting.

**The Dessert Fridge (Front Stage — The Neutral Zone):** Technically a display case. Functionally, a ten-second escape hatch. When a character is overwhelmed mid-rush, they "check the pastries" — crouching to the fridge, making a small show of inventory, buying themselves a breath. The fridge is the only Front Stage location where you can be visible _and_ unavailable. Everyone uses it. No one acknowledges it.

**The Traditional Bar (Back Stage):** Where the qahwa pours happen — long, slow, ceremonial. The pace here is structurally incompatible with the Espresso Bar's pace, which is part of why swapping to Traditional reads, on camera, as a character downshifting on purpose. Heba goes here to reset. Adam goes here to think.

**The Juice Bar (Back Stage):** The blenders are loud. This is the feature, not the bug. Two people standing at the Juice Bar with a blender running can have a conversation that genuinely cannot be overheard from the lobby. The Juice Bar is the closest Qahwtea has to a soundproof room, and it knows it.

**The Sinks (Back Stage — The Confessional):** Tucked next to the Juice Bar, against the back wall. The only true blind spot in the café. If two characters are at the Sinks at the same time, something is being said that will not be said anywhere else in the building. The Sinks are where plot points happen. The audience should learn, by episode three, to lean in whenever the camera finds them there.

---

## Station Fluidity

There are no fixed roles. Over a single shift, any barista may rotate through Espresso → Register → Juice → Sinks → Traditional → back to Espresso, and the rotations are not assigned. They are _chosen_, and the choices are emotional.

A swap is never just a swap. Heba sliding onto Espresso mid-rush means the vibe was crumbling and she came to stabilize it. Adam drifting to the Traditional Bar means his brain hit a logic loop and he needs to run a background process. Moe vanishing to the Sinks after a social peak means he's hit his on-camera ceiling and needs the blind spot. Dianara taking the Register means she's decided the shift needs more laughter and she is going to manufacture it.

The audience learns to read swaps the way they'd read stage blocking. By mid-season, a swap is a line of dialogue.

## Key Props and Their Significance

**The La Marzocco:** Not just equipment — a standard, a metronome, and the show's tension timer. When it's pulling well, the room breathes with it. When it's silent, something has just happened or is about to. Heba calibrates it every opening shift. Adam has watched this calibration process seven times. He has not mentioned this. She knows.

**The Bluetooth Remote (The Qahwtea Frequency):** A small black media controller clipped to the bar backsplash. It controls the playlist, and the playlist is the shift's emotional weather report. On good days it runs warm — Nour, Fairouz, jazz that doesn't try too hard. On hard days it goes quiet. The French indie incident — Adam's accidental contribution, early in his tenure — is now considered a formative lore event and the benchmark for what a "Shift Reset" sounds like. Whoever controls the remote controls the room. Most days, that's Heba. Some days, Lavanya overrides it for a bit she's filming.

**The Oat Cartons (Moe's Shield):** Stacked in the back, restocked Tuesdays and Fridays. Moe builds physical barriers out of oat cartons when he's buffering or cam-shy — a small wall of inventory between himself and the lens, framed as restocking. The Great Oat Shortage (October 14) is in the cast's shared history as a stress test; Adam knocked over a display of six cartons during peak rush on the same date, which is either a coincidence or a folk tale, depending on who's telling it. The cartons are a recurring dramatic prop. Their physical state is a legible record of the day.

**Lavanya's Phone (The Fourth Wall):** When the phone is down, everyone is just working. When the phone comes up, the role shifts — "Barista" becomes "Performance," and every character has a different tell for the transition. Adam stiffens. Moe finds something to crouch behind. Dianara escalates. Heba does not change at all, which is its own kind of change. Lavanya's direction is usually one sentence: _"Do that again, it's for the Monday dump."_

**The Hand-Lettered Menu Board:** Updated with irregular frequency by whoever opens. The font changes depending on who wrote it. Heba's handwriting is steady and even. Dianara's has hearts in it sometimes. Adam has never written the menu board. He has been handed the chalk once and handed it back.

## Time-of-Day Atmosphere

**6:45–7:45 AM (Pre-Rush):** The café belongs to whoever opened. Quiet, mechanical, purposeful. The smell of cleaning product fading under coffee. The light is blue through the east windows. The La Marzocco is being calibrated. Nothing is being filmed.

**7:45–9:15 AM (The Crucible):** Rush hour. The Cummings Research Park crowd arrives in waves — aerospace, defense contractors, startup founders, NASA adjacents — all under-caffeinated, all overcommitted. Orders stack. The machine runs hot. Someone always orders something complicated at exactly the wrong window. Station swaps come fast and the reasons for them are visible to anyone who knows how to read them. This is when characters are revealed.

**9:15–11:30 AM (Post-Rush):** Decompression. The café half-empties. Staff move slower. The music matters more. Conversations that happen in this window are not necessarily quieter — they are, structurally, more possible.

**11:30 AM–2:00 PM (The Real Talk):** Qahwtea's true private hours. A few regulars, laptop workers, long stretches where the lobby goes silent. The Back Stage gets used more — the Juice Bar runs without anyone needing juice, the Sinks see traffic that has nothing to do with dishes. This is when things are said. Not everything important. But most things important.

**2:00–4:00 PM (Afternoon):** Quieter. The light starts to change. Lavanya reviews footage. Someone waters the plants, eventually.

**4:00 PM (The Rose-Gold Hour):** The western windows go warm and the whole bar — marble, chrome, glass — turns shimmering pink-gold. This is the show's beauty-shot window. If a scene needs to look like the characters are living inside a feeling, set it here. This is not coincidental to the show's palette. Lavanya planned it.

**4:00–5:00 PM (Second Wave):** The afternoon fix crowd. Smaller, calmer, more regulars than strangers. The shift is winding down and everyone knows it.

## Atmosphere Notes for Scene-Setting

- When the La Marzocco is silent: something has just happened, or is about to.
- When the playlist skips: involuntary reset. Everyone notices. No one says anything.
- When two characters are at the Sinks: lean in.
- When the blenders run with no juice order pending: a conversation is being hidden.
- When Moe restocks oat cartons during a non-restock window: he's buffering.
- When Adam swaps to Traditional mid-rush: his code is broken.
- When Heba takes the Espresso Bar without being asked: the vibe was about to collapse and she caught it.
- When Dianara laughs for longer than the moment warranted: she is doing emotional labor on the room and it is working.
- When Lavanya lowers the phone mid-bit: the bit was real and she knew it before anyone else did.
- The pothos near the Traditional Bar needs watering. Someone remembers on Thursdays. The someone varies.
